Biryani

History:Veg biryani has its roots in the Mughal era, where aromatic rice dishes were created by combining spices, herbs, and meat. Over time, vegetarian versions became popular across India, especially in regions like Hyderabad, Lucknow, and South India. It evolved into a festive dish known for its rich flavor, layered cooking, and fragrant basmati rice.


Recipe:To make veg biryani, basmati rice is partially cooked and layered with sautéed vegetables like potatoes, carrots, beans, and peas along with biryani masala, yogurt, and fried onions. The layers are sealed and cooked on low heat (dum) until the rice absorbs all the flavors. Garnished with coriander, mint, and a little ghee, veg biryani is served hot with raita for a complete, aromatic meal.

Dal Makhani

History:Dal Makhani originated in Punjab and became famous for its rich, creamy taste. Traditionally cooked slowly overnight, it was made using whole black lentils and kidney beans, giving it a deep, earthy flavor. Over time, it became a popular dish in North Indian cuisine and is now a restaurant favorite across India and abroad.


Recipe:To make dal makhani, whole black lentils and kidney beans are soaked and cooked until soft, then simmered with butter, tomatoes, ginger, garlic, and mild spices. Fresh cream is added at the end to give it its signature creamy texture. It is slow-cooked to enhance flavor and served hot with naan, roti, or rice.

Samosa

History:The samosa originally came from the Middle East and Central Asia, where it was known as “sambosa.” It reached India through traders centuries ago and evolved into the crispy, potato-filled snack we know today. Over time, it became a popular street food across India, enjoyed for its spicy filling and crunchy triangular shape.


Recipe:To make a classic Indian samosa, a dough is prepared using flour, oil, salt, and water. A spicy filling of boiled potatoes, peas, cumin, garam masala, and chilies is cooked and cooled. The dough is rolled into small discs, cut into halves, shaped into cones, filled with the mixture, and sealed. The samosas are then deep-fried until golden and crisp, and served hot with chutney.

Dosa

History:Dosa originated in South India, with its roots tracing back over a thousand years. It began as a simple fermented rice-and-lentil batter cooked on a hot stone. Over centuries, dosa evolved into many varieties and became a staple breakfast dish across India, loved for its crisp texture and light flavor.


Recipe:To make dosa, a batter is prepared by soaking rice and urad dal, then grinding and fermenting it overnight. The fermented batter is spread thinly on a hot pan to create a crisp, golden crepe. It is cooked with a little oil and served hot with coconut chutney and sambar for a traditional South Indian meal.

Pav Bhaji

History:Pav bhaji originated in Mumbai in the mid-1800s as a quick, filling meal for textile mill workers. Vendors mixed leftover vegetables with spices to create a flavorful bhaji and served it with butter-toasted pav (bread rolls). Over time, it became a popular Indian street food enjoyed across the country.


Recipe:To make pav bhaji, mixed vegetables like potatoes, tomatoes, peas, and capsicum are boiled and mashed, then cooked with butter, onions, garlic, and pav bhaji masala. The spicy bhaji is simmered until thick and flavorful. Pav buns are toasted with butter on a pan and served hot with the bhaji, onions, lemon, and coriander.

Shahi Paneer

History:Shahi Paneer originated in the royal kitchens of the Mughal era, where rich, creamy dishes were created using nuts, cream, and aromatic spices. The word “Shahi” means royal, reflecting the dish’s luxurious taste and texture. Over time, it became a popular North Indian curry enjoyed in restaurants and homes for its smooth, mildly sweet flavor


Recipe:To make Shahi Paneer, paneer cubes are added to a creamy gravy made from blended onions, tomatoes, cashews, cream, and mild spices like cardamom and garam masala. The gravy is cooked until smooth and rich, then finished with a little butter or cream for extra flavor. It is served hot with naan, roti, or jeera rice.

Indian
Chole Bhature

History:Chole Bhature originated in North India, especially Punjab, and became popular as a hearty, flavorful dish enjoyed for breakfast or lunch. The combination of spicy chickpea curry (chole) and deep-fried fluffy bread (bhature) grew in popularity across India due to its rich taste and satisfying portions. Today, it is a famous street food and restaurant dish loved for its bold flavors.


Recipe: To make Chole Bhature, chickpeas are cooked with onions, tomatoes, ginger, garlic, and spices like chole masala, cumin, and coriander to create a thick, spicy curry. Bhature dough is made using flour, yogurt, salt, and a little oil, then rested, rolled, and deep-fried until puffed and golden. The dish is served hot with onions, lemon, and pickles for a classic North Indian meal.
